The Rulebook

How the house plays it

Standard Chicken Foot with a few family twists baked in. Here's everything that matters at the table.

🁫

The set & the goal

A double-twelve set (91 tiles), seven dealt to each player. You play thirteen rounds and add up leftover pips each round — the lowest total after the last round wins the match.

🎯

Counting down the doubles

Round 1 opens on the 12-12, round 2 on the 11-11, and so on down to the 0-0. Whoever holds that round's double sets it in the center; if nobody holds it, the highest double held opens instead.

✦

Four arms from the center

The opening double is special: it sits in the middle and opens four directions at once. Unlike every later double, the center one needs no chicken foot — all four arms are live immediately.

🦶

The chicken foot

Play any other double and it must be satisfied first: the next three tiles branch off it as legs. Until all three legs are down, that's the only place anyone may play.

🔄

Draw one, then pass

Stuck? Draw a single tile from the boneyard. Play it if you can; otherwise you pass. You only pass outright when the boneyard is empty.

🏁

Ending a round

The round ends the instant someone empties their hand. Everyone else totals the dots left in hand and adds them to their score. If it blocks, both hands count.

The wild 12s — every tile bearing a 12 except the 12-12 is wild. A wild plays on any open end: the 12 matches whatever's there, and the next player works off the tile's other half. The 12-12 is never wild — it's a normal double that springs a chicken foot. (That's twelve wild bones in the set: 12-0 through 12-11.)

The Story

About this table

A family rulebook, made playable

Chicken Foot is a classic of the domino world, but every household bends it. This version captures one family's house rules exactly: the four-armed center opener, the countdown through every double, the draw-one-then-pass turn, and the signature twist — the wild twelves.

It's built as a single page with no accounts, no tracking, and nothing saved between visits. Just open it and play.

Do I need the physical set?

No — everything's on screen. But the rules map one-to-one to a real double-twelve set if you want to play at the table.

How hard is the bot?

It dumps heavy tiles, hoards its wilds, and pounces on chicken feet. Beatable, but it plays a tidy game.

Why do rounds sometimes block?

With only seven tiles each from a 91-tile set, open ends often can't be matched. When both players pass in a row, the round blocks and both hands are counted.

Can two people play?

Right now it's you versus the bot. A pass-and-play mode for two humans can be added easily.
